1. **Objective** — what this module builds
2. **Estimated time** — how long to budget

5. **Common mistakes** — what goes wrong and how to catch it
6. **Quick self-check** — prove it before you move on
7. **Coach sign-off + notes** — make the progress official

**How to progress:** Complete Block 1 (Foundations) in order before starting Block 2 (Reading the Game). Do not skip the self-checks — they are the product of the module, not an afterthought.

## MODULE 1 — Stance & Ready Position

**Objective:** Build a repeatable, balanced stance you can hold under fatigue and return to after every movement or save.

**Estimated time:** 60–75 minutes on ice + 15 minutes workbook

[INSERT VIDEO: stance overview and demonstration]

### Step-by-step instructions

1. **Set the base.** Skates slightly wider than shoulder width, weight on the balls of the feet, heels light. Bounce once — if you tip forward or back, reset.
2. **Stack the joints.** Knees bend forward over the toes, hips sit back slightly, chest stays up. Ankles, knees, and hips flex together.
3. **Set the hands.** Glove and blocker in front of the body, visible in your bottom peripheral vision. Glove open and angled to the shooter, blocker square.
4. **Set the stick.** Blade flat on the ice, centered between the skates, 6–12 inches ahead of the toes. Paddle angled so pucks deflect to the corners, not straight back.
5. **Hold and scan.** Hold the stance for 30 seconds while moving only your eyes across three targets. Rest, repeat 5 times.
6. **Stress it.** Have a coach or partner give light pushes on the shoulders and hips. If you stumble, your base is too narrow or your weight is on your heels.
7. **Return to it.** Do 10 reps of: drop to butterfly → recover → land back in your exact stance. The stance you recover into must match the stance you started in.

[INSERT IMAGE: side view of correct stance with joint-stacking lines]
[INSERT IMAGE: front view showing hand and stick position]

### Coaching cues

- "Chest up, hips down."
- "Hands you can see."
- "Toes loaded, heels light."
- "Same stance every time."

### Common mistakes

- **Hands tucked at the hips** — pucks beat you before the glove moves. Hands live in front.
- **Weight on the heels** — first push is slow and you fall off shots. Feel pressure in the balls of the feet.
- **Chest collapsed forward** — kills sightlines and balance. Bend at the knees and ankles, not the waist.
- **Stance changes under fatigue** — the last rep should look like the first. If it doesn't, shorten the set.

- [ ] I can hold my stance for 30 seconds without shifting weight
- [ ] My hands are visible in my bottom peripheral vision
- [ ] I recover from the butterfly into the same stance 10/10 times
- [ ] A partner's push does not knock me off balance

## MODULE 2 — Crease Movement & Skating

**Objective:** Move around the crease with control — shuffle, T-push, and butterfly slide — arriving set and on balance before the shot, not during it.

**Estimated time:** 75–90 minutes on ice + 15 minutes workbook

[INSERT VIDEO: crease movement overview]

### Step-by-step instructions

1. **Shuffle — short lateral moves.** From your stance, push off the far leg and step the lead skate 6–12 inches, staying square to the puck the whole time. Head and chest stay level — no bobbing. 3 sets of 10 shuffles each direction.
2. **T-push — longer lateral moves.** Rotate the lead skate toward your target, push hard off the back leg, glide, then snap back square and set. Eyes lock the destination before the push. 3 sets of 8 each direction.
3. **Butterfly slide — moving while down.** From the butterfly, load the push leg, rotate the lead pad toward the target, push and slide with the chest facing the puck. Recover to stance. 3 sets of 6 each direction.
4. **Post-to-post circuit.** Combine: shuffle to center, T-push to far post, butterfly slide back to center, recover. 5 circuits each direction at game speed.
5. **Stop on arrival.** On every movement, finish with a hard inside-edge stop and a full second of stillness in your stance before the next rep. Arriving set is the skill.

[INSERT VIDEO: T-push technique]
[INSERT VIDEO: butterfly slide technique]
[INSERT IMAGE: crease movement pattern diagram — post to post routes]

### Coaching cues

- "Set before the shot."
- "Lead with the eyes, then the feet."
- "Stay square through the shuffle."
- "Push, glide, stop, still."

### Common mistakes

- **Drifting past the spot** — momentum carries you off your angle. Finish every move with a hard stop.
- **Opening up on the shuffle** — turning into a lazy T-push leaves holes mid-move. Shoulders and hips stay square.
- **Head bobbing** — your eyes lose the puck every time your head drops. Level head, quiet upper body.
- **Sliding when a shuffle would do** — going down early to move takes away your feet. Stay on your skates unless the play demands the slide.

### Quick self-check

- [ ] I can shuffle post-to-post staying square the entire way
- [ ] My T-pushes finish with a controlled stop, not a drift
- [ ] I complete 5 post-to-post circuits arriving set every time
- [ ] I choose shuffle vs. T-push vs. slide correctly on command

## MODULE 3 — Angles & Depth Management

**Objective:** Position yourself on the shooter's line and at the right depth so shots hit you — cutting down the net before the puck ever leaves the stick.

**Estimated time:** 60–75 minutes on ice + 20 minutes workbook

[INSERT VIDEO: angles and depth overview]

### Step-by-step instructions

1. **Find center.** Learn to locate the middle of your net without looking back: use the crease markings, posts touched with the stick or glove, and ice landmarks. 10 reps of skating out and returning to dead center — check yourself by glancing back only *after* you've committed.
2. **Draw the line.** Imagine a straight line from the puck to the middle of the net. Your job is to put your chest on that line. Have a coach move a puck around the zone slowly while you track and adjust; check with a stick laid from the puck to the goal line.
3. **Set your depth ladder.** Define three depths: **top of the crease** (shot threat, no pass option), **mid-crease** (shot threat with a pass option), **near the goal line** (play behind or beside the net). Walk each depth 5 times so each one is a known, felt position.
4. **Angle circuit.** Coach walks the puck through five stations (corner, half-wall, point, slot, opposite corner). At each station you re-find your line and call out your depth choice. 3 circuits.
5. **Add shots.** Repeat the circuit with real shots. If the puck hits you without a reaching save, the angle was right. Track how many of 15 shots hit your body.

[INSERT IMAGE: overhead diagram — puck-to-net lines from five shooting positions]
[INSERT IMAGE: depth ladder diagram — top of crease, mid-crease, goal line]

### Coaching cues

- "Chest on the line."
- "Shots should hit you."
- "Depth follows the pass threat."
- "Know your net without looking."

### Common mistakes

- **Squaring to the shooter's body instead of the puck** — the puck scores, not the player. Line up on the release point.
- **Playing one depth everywhere** — too deep gives away net; too aggressive dies on the pass. Pick depth from the ladder every time.
- **Peeking at the net mid-play** — every glance back is a lost half-second on the puck. Trust your landmarks.
- **Correcting angle during the shot** — if you're still moving at release, you're late. Win the angle early.

### Quick self-check

- [ ] I can return to dead center 10/10 times without looking back
- [ ] 12 of 15 clean shots hit my body without a reaching save
- [ ] I can name and demonstrate my three depths on command
- [ ] I stay set at release on every station of the circuit

## MODULE 4 — Rebound Control

**Objective:** Decide where every puck goes after it hits you — smothered, steered to the corner, or directed to a teammate — so second chances disappear.

**Estimated time:** 75–90 minutes on ice + 15 minutes workbook

[INSERT VIDEO: rebound control overview]

### Step-by-step instructions

1. **Sort every save into three outcomes.** Before each drill rep, call your intention out loud: **"Freeze"** (chest/glove, play dead), **"Corner"** (steer wide of the slot), or **"Play"** (set it for your stick or a teammate). Intention first, save second.
2. **Stick saves to the corner.** Shooter fires low shots on the ice. Angle the stick blade so the puck deflects to the corner boards, not straight back into the slot. 20 reps each side.
3. **Pad saves with direction.** On butterfly saves, rotate the pad's face toward the corner at contact — a flat pad returns the puck to the shooter. 20 reps each side.
4. **Chest saves that die.** On shots into the body, exhale and cradle — trap the puck against the chest and cover immediately. No puck should drop more than a pad-length in front of you. 15 reps.
5. **Glove shots you keep.** Catch and show control — squeeze, look it into the glove, hold. 15 reps.
6. **Chaos test.** Shooter fires 15 mixed shots at game pace. Score each rebound: 2 points = intended outcome, 1 point = safe but unintended, 0 points = rebound into the slot. Target: 24+/30.

[INSERT VIDEO: steering low shots with the stick]
[INSERT VIDEO: pad rotation on butterfly saves]
[INSERT VIDEO: chest save absorption technique]
[INSERT IMAGE: rebound zone map — safe zones vs. danger zone]

### Coaching cues

- "Call it before you catch it."
- "Corners, never the slot."
- "Soft chest, hard squeeze."
- "The save isn't over until the puck is dead or gone."

### Common mistakes

- **Stopping the puck without a plan** — a save into the slot is half a goal against. Every rep starts with an intention.
- **Flat stick blade** — returns shots straight back to the shooter. Angle the blade before the shot arrives.
- **Rigid chest on body shots** — pucks bounce off a tense torso. Absorb, then cover.
- **Admiring the save** — the rebound is live while you watch it. Eyes find the puck the instant it leaves you.

### Quick self-check

- [ ] I call my intended outcome before every rep
- [ ] Low stick saves reach the corner boards 8/10 times per side
- [ ] Body shots die within a pad-length 13/15 times
- [ ] I score 24+/30 on the chaos test

## MODULE 5 — Reading the Release

**Objective:** Pick up shot cues from the shooter's body and stick *before* the puck leaves, so your save starts early instead of on time.

**Estimated time:** 60–75 minutes on ice + 20 minutes workbook (plus video study)

[INSERT VIDEO: reading the release overview]

### Step-by-step instructions

1. **Learn the tells.** Study the three big pre-shot cues: **blade angle** (open = high, closed = low), **hand position** (hands away from the body = shot coming), and **weight shift** (loading the back leg = wrist/snap shot, big windup = slapper). Watch each on video before taking the ice.
2. **Call the shot.** Shooter takes 20 shots at half speed. Your only job: call "high" or "low" out loud *before release*. Don't save it — read it. Track your accuracy.
3. **Read, then save.** Repeat at 3/4 speed, now making the save. The read should make you feel early. 20 reps.
4. **Shot vs. pass.** Add a second shooter. The puck carrier either shoots or passes; call "shot" or "pass" before it happens. Hands wide and blade closed usually mean pass. 15 reps.
5. **Screen reads.** Put a body in front. When you lose the puck, read the shooter's posture and blade through or around the screen. 10 reps.
6. **Video habit.** After sessions, watch 5 minutes of game footage and pause at each release: could you have called it? Log two reads you got right and one you missed.

[INSERT VIDEO: blade angle cues — open vs. closed comparison]
[INSERT VIDEO: shot vs. pass body language]
[INSERT IMAGE: annotated photo — shooter pre-release cues labeled]

### Coaching cues

- "Read the blade, not the puck."
- "Call it before it leaves."
- "Early beats fast."
- "Posture tells the truth."

### Common mistakes

- **Staring at the puck alone** — the puck tells you nothing until it's moving; the blade and body tell you first. Widen your focus.
- **Guessing instead of reading** — cheating to one side is not a read. If you can't call it, stay square and trust your positioning.
- **Reading late** — a read after release is just a reaction. The call must come while the puck is still on the blade.
- **Skipping the video work** — reads are built off-ice as much as on-ice. Do the film habit.

### Quick self-check

- [ ] I call high/low correctly on 15/20 half-speed shots
- [ ] I call shot/pass correctly on 11/15 reps
- [ ] My saves feel early, not desperate, at 3/4 speed
- [ ] I've logged reads from at least two film sessions

## MODULE 6 — Puck Tracking

**Objective:** Keep your eyes attached to the puck from release to contact — leading every save with your head so your body follows in sequence.

**Estimated time:** 60–75 minutes on ice + 15 minutes workbook

[INSERT VIDEO: puck tracking overview]

### Step-by-step instructions

1. **Eye-attach warm-up.** Partner tosses tennis balls from 10 feet. Catch each one while watching it all the way into your hand — literally see the contact. 20 tosses. This is the feeling you're chasing with pucks.
2. **Track into the body.** Shooter fires soft mid-speed shots into your chest and pads. Watch each puck all the way to contact; nod your head down as it arrives. 20 reps.
3. **Head leads the save.** On lateral shots, your head and eyes move to the puck first, then hands, then body. Exaggerate the head turn on 15 glove-side and 15 blocker-side reps.
4. **Track through traffic.** Add a screen. Fight for sightlines — look over, around, or under the screen; move your head, not just your body. When you find the puck late, attach hard. 15 reps.
5. **Rebound re-attach.** Every drill above ends only when your eyes find the puck *after* the save. Save → find it → attach. Make the re-find automatic.
6. **Off-ice habit.** 5 minutes daily: juggling, ball-wall tracking, or reaction-ball work, always watching the object into the hand.

[INSERT VIDEO: head trajectory on lateral saves — slow motion]
[INSERT VIDEO: tracking through screens]
[INSERT IMAGE: photo sequence — eyes attached from release to chest contact]

### Coaching cues

- "See it hit you."
- "Head first, hands second."
- "Nod it into the chest."
- "Find it again — every time."

### Common mistakes

- **Watching the general area** — "seeing" the shot is not tracking it. Attach to the puck itself, all the way in.
- **Pulling the head off the puck** — flinching or turning away breaks the save sequence. The head finishes toward the puck, never away.
- **Body beats the eyes** — moving before your eyes have the puck means you're guessing. Eyes, head, hands, body — in that order.
- **Losing the puck after the save** — a stopped puck you can't see is a live threat. Re-attach instantly.

### Quick self-check

- [ ] I see the puck contact my body on 18/20 tracking reps
- [ ] My head visibly leads on lateral saves (confirmed on video)
- [ ] I re-find the puck within one second on every save
- [ ] I'm doing 5 minutes of off-ice tracking daily

## MODULE 7 — Post Play

**Objective:** Own the posts — seal them on sharp-angle attacks and wraparounds, and move in and out of post integrations without opening holes.

**Estimated time:** 75–90 minutes on ice + 15 minutes workbook

[INSERT VIDEO: post play overview]

### Step-by-step instructions

1. **Learn your seals.** Practice two positions until they're automatic: **RVH (reverse-VH)** — post-side pad flush on the ice sealing the bottom, skate against the post, body rotated toward the play; and **stand-up post lean** — skate locked to the post, body upright and square for plays farther out. 10 slow entries into each on both posts.
2. **Pick by threat.** RVH when the puck is below the goal line or tight to the post; stay on your skates when the shooter is above the face-off dot. Coach calls puck positions, you call and take the right seal. 15 reps per side.
3. **Enter and exit clean.** The hole opens during the transition. Drill: stance at the top of the crease → play moves behind the net → drop into RVH → play comes back out front → push out to your angle. No gap between pad and post at any point. 10 circuits each side.
4. **Wraparound battles.** Coach carries the puck behind the net and attacks either post. Seal before the puck arrives — post-to-post through the crease, not around the top. 10 reps each direction.
5. **Sharp-angle shots.** Shooter attacks from below the dot. Absorb short-side shots with the seal; on jam plays, stay patient and let them run out of net. 15 reps per side.
6. **Escape to the slot.** From RVH, the pass goes to the slot — push off the post explosively into your angle and set. 10 reps each side.

[INSERT VIDEO: wraparound coverage both directions]

[INSERT IMAGE: diagram — when to use RVH vs. stand-up post lean]

### Coaching cues

- "Seal before the puck arrives."
- "No daylight at the post."
- "Patience on the jam."
- "Post is home, not a hiding place."

### Common mistakes

- **Living in the RVH** — dropping into the seal when the shooter is out high gives away the top of the net. Match the seal to the threat.
- **Gap between pad and post** — a moving transition that opens a pad-width of daylight is where wraparounds score. Slow it down until the seal never breaks.
- **Getting beat around the top** — going over the top on wraparounds is slower than through the crease. Post-to-post stays low and direct.
- **Stuck on the post** — sealing is temporary; when the play leaves the post, you leave too. Explode back to your angle.

### Quick self-check

- [ ] My RVH shows no daylight at either post (confirmed by coach or video)
- [ ] I choose the correct seal on 13/15 called threats per side
- [ ] I stop 8/10 wraparound attempts each direction
- [ ] I exit the post to a set angle within one second of the pass

## MODULE 8 — Puck Handling

**Objective:** Turn dump-ins and loose pucks into possession for your team — stopping rims, setting pucks for defensemen, and making simple, safe passes under pressure.

**Estimated time:** 75–90 minutes on ice + 15 minutes workbook

[INSERT VIDEO: puck handling overview]

### Step-by-step instructions

1. **Grip and stance.** Blocker hand stays at the top of the paddle; glove hand grips lower on the shaft (goalie "power grip") for passes and clears. Practice switching from save grip to handling grip and back — 20 smooth switches before touching a puck.
2. **Stop the rim.** Coach rims pucks around the boards. Leave the crease, set your stick blade against the boards angled to kill the rim, stop the puck flat. 15 reps each direction.
3. **Set it for your D.** After each stop, leave the puck a stick-length off the boards on the strong side, then return to the crease. A good set is one your defenseman can take at speed without breaking stride. 15 reps.
4. **Forehand pass.** Short, firm passes to a target 15–20 feet away — cushion the puck, look at your target, follow through low. 20 reps.
5. **Backhand clear.** Off the boards and out — height and hardness beat precision when under pressure. 15 reps.
6. **Decision drill.** Coach dumps the puck and calls "time" (set it), "pressure" (rim it out or leave it), or "pass" (find the outlet). Wrong option under pressure is worse than no touch at all. 15 reps.
7. **Get back home.** Every rep ends with a hard return to the crease and a set stance. Handling the puck is only a win if you're back in the net when the play returns.

### Coaching cues

- "Stop it flat, leave it clean."
- "Simple beats fancy."
- "When in doubt, rim it out."
- "Touch the puck, then get home."

### Common mistakes

- **Wandering without a plan** — leaving the net to "help" with no read invites the empty-net goal. Decide before you leave the crease.
- **Overhandling** — extra touches under pressure turn possession into turnovers. One stop, one decision, one touch when possible.
- **Soft board sets** — a puck bouncing off the boards into the slot is a gift. Kill the rim dead before you set it.
- **Slow return to the net** — a great set with an empty net behind it is a bad trade. Sprint home, set your stance.

### Quick self-check

- [ ] I stop rims cleanly 12/15 times each direction
- [ ] My defenseman can take 12/15 of my sets without slowing down
- [ ] I make the correct call on 12/15 decision-drill reps
- [ ] I'm back in the crease and set within 3 seconds on every rep
